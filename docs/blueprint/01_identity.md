# Project Axiom — Identity (01_identity.md)

This document defines the core purpose and strategic coordinates of Project Axiom.

---

## 1. Product Statement

> **"This is the digital logbook of an engineer who resolves the architecture before writing the code."**

### Why this works:
It establishes an immediate hierarchy of value. It does not say "I am a fullstack developer." It positions code as the final, simple act of a much larger, more disciplined thinking process.

### The Challenge:
Many developers claim they "think before they build." If we write this, we must prove it. Our case files cannot just be lists of technologies; they must be structured records of failed hypotheses, trace analysis, and architectural constraints. If the content doesn't back this statement up, it will read as empty marketing.

---

## 2. Vision

### **"To replace the standard developer resume with a demonstration of engineering judgment."**

* The resume is a list of tools and dates. It is transactional.
* Project Axiom is a record of decision-making. It is strategic.
* We want to shift the evaluation of a developer from *what* they know to *how* they navigate trade-offs under constraints.

---

## 3. The Target Audience

We are not designing for HR algorithms or recruiters looking to match keyword buzzwords. We are designing for:

1. **The Technical Founder / CTO**: The person who has built systems that collapsed under their own weight, who knows the cost of "fast, sloppy code," and who is looking for a developer they can trust with core database schemas and system integrations.
2. **The Product Architect**: The peer who values restraint, who wants to read case files that show real engineering trade-offs (e.g., event sourcing vs. queuing latency), and who respects someone who documents their failures as clearly as their successes.

### Audience Constraint:
These people have high bullshit detectors. They will immediately spot generic statements like "I am passionate about clean code." Every claim must be backed by a precise engineering observation.

---

## 4. Long-Term Purpose

To serve as a persistent reference point for how engineering *should* be documented. The site is a living field notebook that remains relevant years after it is written, because principles do not expire.

---

## 5. Success Criteria

* **The Time Metric**: A visitor spends 5+ minutes reading the text rather than scanning for images or logos.
* **The Cognitive Shift**: A visitor closes the browser and remembers the *investigation structure* of a project, not the fact that it was built with React or Node.
* **The Call to Action**: The inbound emails you receive do not ask "Do you know technology X?" Instead, they ask: "How would you approach this database bottleneck we are facing?"

---

## 6. Brand Personality Coordinates

We align the identity with brands and objects that define craftsmanship through restraint:

* **Leica (Tactile Restraint)**: Focuses the user on the essentials by stripping away automation. It is manual, mechanical, and quiet.
* **Porsche (Functional Silhouette)**: The engineering constraint is the signature. Evolution over disruption.
* **Dieter Rams (Less, But Better)**: Good design is as little design as possible. The interface disappears to let the utility shine.
* **Editorial journals / Field Notebooks**: Unadorned, structured, monospaced layout elements, utilizing white space as a structural material.

---

## Creative Director Questions for the Builder

Before we proceed to `02_story.md`, I need you to challenge this identity:

1. **Where is the friction?** A premium experience requires friction. If the site is too easy to skim, it fails the "Leica" rule. Should we intentionally hide or gate information to force the reader to interact like an investigator?
2. **What are we willing to lose?** By targeting CTOs and technical founders who value deep systems thinking, we will alienate recruiters who search for keyword lists. Are you fully comfortable with a design that makes no concessions to the standard recruitment pipeline?
3. **Is "Software Engineer" the right title?** If our statement is about resolving architecture before code, does the label "Software Engineer" capture that, or is it too generic? What about "Systems Builder" or "Systems Architect"?
